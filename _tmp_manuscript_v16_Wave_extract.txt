P0009	Abstract:
P0010	Adjusting solution pH to a target value is a routine operation in chemical experiments, yet a tedious trial-and-error process, especially for multicomponent chemical systems with unknown and nonlinear buffering behavior. Here, we reformulate pH adjustment as a sequential inference and decision-making problem and develop a hybrid framework that converts this empirical procedure into a systematic, knowledge-driven process. Bayesian inference is used to extract the buffering properties of the solution from experimental feedback for rule-based pH control. This interpretable but calculation-costly model was then distilled into a neural policy via imitation learning for rapid and transferable control, and further refined through reinforcement learning to improve robustness under model mismatch and uncertainty. Across 3,000 simulated tasks involving mono- and polyprotic systems, this approach achieves a 94.30% success rate with an average of 10.22±7.24 steps, outperforming the rule-based controller (12.81±7.68, 94.23% success rate) while reducing decision latency. Deployment on a robotic platform demonstrates consistent performance across diverse chemical systems, including mixed buffers, wastewater neutralization, protein precipitation, and metal–ligand coordination, achieving target pH within ±0.1 units. This work establishes a general strategy for transforming pH adjustment from empirical trial-and-error into a structured and learnable chemical decision process.
P0011	MAIN TEXT
P0012	Introduction
P0013	Precise control of pH is essential in a wide range of chemical, biological, and manufacturing processes, yet its practical implementation remains fundamentally empirical1-3. In typical laboratory operation, pH adjustment is performed through sequential addition of acid or base while observing the system response, with decisions guided by experience rather than explicit models. This trial-and-error procedure arises because the effective buffering behavior of real solutions—often composed of multiple weak acids, bases, and interacting species—is unknown and highly nonlinear4, 5. Each adjustment step therefore acts not only as a control action but also as a probe of the underlying chemical system, from which operators implicitly infer how the system will respond to subsequent perturbations. 1, 6, 7
P0014	Despite its ubiquity, pH adjustment has rarely been formalized as a structured inference and decision-making problem8, 9. Classical control strategies, such as proportional–integral–derivative (PID)10, 11 assume fixed system dynamics, and often degrade when applied to chemically heterogeneous environments. Data-driven approaches, including reinforcement learning1213, offer flexibility but typically require extensive sampling and lack interpretability, limiting their applicability in chemically diverse settings. As a result, there remains no general framework that can both extract chemically meaningful system representations from limited observations and use them to guide efficient and adaptive control.
P0015	Here, we address this challenge by explicitly reformulating pH adjustment as a sequential inference process over an unknown chemical system, coupled to a decision policy that evolves with acquired knowledge. In this perspective, each addition of reagent updates an internal representation of the system’s buffering characteristics, which in turn informs subsequent actions. This view enables a transition from heuristic, experience-based adjustment to a systematic, knowledge-driven approach in which chemical behavior is progressively learned, represented, and exploited for control.
P0016	To realize this framework, we develop a hybrid approach that integrates interpretable chemical modelling with data-driven policy learning. Bayesian inference is first used to estimate effective buffer parameters from experimental feedback, constructing an explicit and continuously updated representation of the system. These inferred parameters were used to guide rule-based dosing decisions. Then the resulting decision logic is distilled into a neural network through imitation learning, allowing rapid and generalizable action selection without repeated inference. Finally, reinforcement learning is employed to refine the policy under conditions where the simplified chemical model is incomplete, enabling adaptation to unmodeled complexities and experimental noise. Through this staged process, chemical knowledge is first inferred, then compressed, and ultimately optimized for deployment, providing a general strategy for transforming empirical laboratory procedures into systematic and learnable processes.

The method is quite robust and tractable, but it requires repeated equilibrium calculations, sampling a large number of parameters for distributions, and large amount of parameter updates at each step, which is computationally demanding and slow like other Bayesian inference method, and thus is difficult to be implemented in real-time operation.
P0032	3. Learning a Fast Control Policy by Imitation
P0033	A fast and directly deployable policy can be learnt through imitation learning15 from the above chemically informed control model. A virtual pH-adjustment environment is constructed based on the equilibrium model. Up to three pairs of buffers with preset pKa and concentration were put into the environment, so the pH change after adding a certain volume of acid or base can be calculated exactly. The Bayesian controller is used to generate adjustment trajectories in this environment, where each step records the current system state and the corresponding dosing decision. For the imitation learning, the state is defined using quantities directly accessible during operation, including the current pH, the target pH, their deviation, and recent response information, while the action corresponds to the dosing volume determined by the model-informed control rule.
P0034	A neural network is trained on these state–action pairs to reproduce the dosing decisions generated by the Bayesian controller. The resulting network after training on 3000 simulated pH adjustment curves, serves as an efficient policy execution, enabling rapid and stable pH adjustment. The efficacy of this imitation-learning neural network can also be evaluated in the virtue environment. If the imitation-learning model can successfully adjust pH from the initial value to the targeted value (±0.1) within 50 steps, we record the process as a successful trajectory. Compared with the Bayesian rule-based baseline, the imitation-learning network maintained a similar success rate (93.87% vs. 94.23%), with fewer average steps (10.3 vs. 12.8), a lower overshoot rate (31.25% vs. 42.70%), and a similar step-count standard deviation (7.68 vs. 7.21).
P0035	4. Policy Optimization through Reinforcement Learning
P0036	While imitation learning provides a fast approximation of the model-informed control strategy, its performance remains fundamentally limited by the efficiency of the original rule-based policy, which is empirically curated and not guaranteed to be optimal. To address this limitation, reinforcement learning (RL)16 is introduced to further refine the control policy through direct interaction with the experimental system. Starting from the policy obtained by imitation learning, the controller is allowed to make incremental adjustments to its dosing decisions and observe the resulting pH response. A reward signal is defined based on the reduction of pH deviation from the target, with additional penalties applied to discourage overshooting and excessive reagent usage. In this way, the controller is encouraged to reach the target pH efficiently while maintaining stability.

P0039	5. Integration with Robotic Execution.
P0040	The trained policy is deployed on a robotic pH- adjustment system equipped with precise syringe pumps and a pH sensor. At each step, the system measures the pH, decides the next reagent volume, executes the addition, and remeasures the pH, until the target pH is reached within a predefined margin (typically ±0.1).
P0041	Experiment 1. Benchmarking Three Control Policies in a Robotic pH Adjustment Task
P0042	We tested the ability of Bayesian rule-based controller, reinforcement learning derived controller, and human operation as a benchmark to adjust the pH of real buffer mixtures to a target value of 6.0 ± 0.1 using only 0.1 M HCl or 0.1 M NaOH as the reagents. Each experiment began with 12 mL of a mixed-buffer solution containing two of the three weak acid pairs (p-hydroxybenzoic acid, terephthalic acid, acetic acid, and sulfosalicylic acid, Mixtures 1-4, Figure 2b), selected to have overlapping buffering zones and nontrivial pH-adjustment curve. The robotic platforms executed acid or base additions in 50 µL increments.
P0043	The Bayesian rule-based controller reached the target pH in 12, 2, 7, and 8 steps for Mixtures 1-4, respectively. Its decisions reflected a cautious, model-updating strategy, especially when it approached the target zone. However, overall operation time was slowed by the computational cost of the inference stage. In comparison, the reinforcement learning–tuned policy achieved comparable trajectories (9, 3, 12, 8 steps for Mixtures 1-4, respectively) and negligible overhead time for decision-making. Human operators reached the target in 4, 8, 5, and 6 steps, respectively. The reinforcement-learned policy not only retains the expert-like behavior encoded by chemical rules but also adapts more flexibly to the unmodeled nuances of real-world systems.

P0045	Experiment 2. Waste Water Neutralization
P0046	Neutralizing laboratory wastewater is an essential safety and compliance step in chemical operations16. We applied the autonomous pH adjustment system with reinforcement learning–based policy to the neutralization of three distinct laboratory wastewater samples with unknown compositions with volumes of 15 mL (Figure 3). The initial pH values varied significantly across the samples. The objective was to adjust each solution to pH 7.0 ± 0.1 using automated additions of 0.1 M HCl or 0.1 M NaOH. The tasks were completed within 13, 14, and 22 steps, respectively. The consistent and efficient convergence further highlights the robustness of the policy under noisy and variable conditions.

P0048	Experiment 3. Isoelectric Precipitation of Casein from Milk
P0049	The isoelectric point (pI) of a protein corresponds to the pH at which its net charge is zero, leading to minimal solubility and enhanced aggregation. Proteins such as casein, the predominant protein in milk (~80%), can be selectively precipitated by adjusting the pH to a pI in the range of 4.6–5.2, which underpins its extraction in dairy processing and protein purification protocols17-19. The autonomous pH adjustment system can adjust the pH of 12 mL of milk using 0.1 M HCl or 0.1 M NaOH within four steps (Figure 4).
P0050	Following pH adjustment, the precipitated casein was isolated via centrifugation, washed with a 1:1 ethanol–ether mixture to remove lipids, and oven-dried at 80 °C to constant weight. The yield was 171 mg from 12 mL of milk. Protein identity was confirmed by redissolving the casein in 3% NaOH and observing a violet color change upon addition of Biuret reagent, indicating the presence of peptide bonds. This experiment demonstrates the ability of the system to handle real-world, nonideal media such as milk and perform functional biochemical separations with high accuracy and reproducibility.

P0052	Experiment 4. Determination of Composition and Stability Constant of the Copper- Sulfosalicylic Acid Complex
P0053	The composition and stability of the Cu²⁺–5-sulfosalicylic acid (SSA) complex were investigated using the method of continuous variation (Job’s method). This approach involves varying the molar ratio of the two components while keeping their total concentration constant, and measuring a physical property—here, the UV-Vis absorbance at the complex’s characteristic wavelength—that reflects the extent of complexation. The maximum in the Job’s plot indicates the dominant stoichiometric ratio20-23.
P0054	A series of mixed solutions with varying Cu²⁺/SSA molar ratios were prepared at a constant total concentration (14.7 mM), and the pH of each solution was adjusted to 4.2 ± 0.1 using the automated control system, under conditions that the 1:1 copper–SSA complex is known to dominate. The pH adjustment profiles (Figure 5) show consistent and stable convergence across all samples, demonstrating the reliability of the control strategy under varying chemical compositions.

P0058	Conclusions
P0059	In this work, we transform pH adjustment from a trial-and-error operation into a systematic, model-informed process. By combining Bayesian inference with equilibrium-based control, the system constructs an explicit and continuously updated representation of the underlying buffering behavior, enabling chemically grounded dosing decisions without prior knowledge of solution composition.
P0060	Building on this foundation, imitation learning converts the model-informed control process into a fast and directly deployable policy, while reinforcement learning further refines the strategy through interaction with real systems, allowing adaptation to model inaccuracies and experimental variability. This multi-stage framework integrates physical modeling and data-driven learning in a complementary manner, achieving both interpretability and efficiency.
P0061	Beyond pH adjustment, the approach provides a general paradigm for automated chemical control in systems where the governing chemistry is only partially known. By explicitly linking inference, decision-making, and adaptive learning, it offers a route toward more autonomous and reliable operation in complex chemical environments.
P0062	Materials and Methods
P0063	Chemical Reagents. All aqueous solutions were prepared using Type I water. A complete list of reagents used in the experiments can be found in SI Section 2.
P0064	Bayesian Parameter Updating
P0065	The process consists of five steps: sampling (drawing parameter combinations from the prior distribution), prediction (calculating the expected pH based on chemical equilibrium, including effective pKa calculation and solving the charge conservation equation), evaluation (calculating likelihood to weight the combinations), resampling (retaining combinations that better match observations based on weights), and statistical updating (computing the mean and standard deviation of the posterior distribution). Through iteration, this improves understanding of the chemical system and enables more precise adjustment strategies. The full details are available in SI Section 4.
P0066	Determining Adjustment Volume Using Bayesian Parameter Updates and Control Logic
P0067	A volume calculation formula is designed, selecting the closest value in a discrete volume space. The calculation incorporates components such as current pH deviation, pH change, rate adjustment term, uncertainty factor, buffer factor, and theoretical volume. These components are integrated and smoothed to obtain the adjustment volume. The theoretical pH is calculated using posterior pKa and associated uncertainty to solve for the volume that meets the target. The full details are available in SI Section 4.
P0068	Imitation Learning
P0069	Successful experimental trajectories are simulated and generated using code based on Bayesian inference. Each trajectory contains multiple state-action pairs. States include current pH, target pH, pH difference, current error, and previous reagent volume; the action is the current reagent volume. Trajectories are split into pairs, shuffled, and divided into datasets. A multilayer perceptron model (with input layer, hidden layers, and output layer) is trained using cross-entropy loss and an optimizer. The model performs excellently on the test set, successfully imitating and surpassing the Bayesian algorithm. The full details are available in SI Section 4.
P0070	Reinforcement Learning for Optimizing Neural Network Parameters
P0071	Based on the imitation model, a Monte Carlo policy gradient algorithm is used for reinforcement learning, guiding model parameter updates through a reward function to increase cumulative returns. The process involves sampling complete trajectories, calculating discounted returns, and optimizing via multiple reward components. After training, an updated policy network is obtained that can generate experimental actions based on the current state. The full details are available in SI Section 4.
